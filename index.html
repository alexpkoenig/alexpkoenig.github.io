A bit about me...

Hello, I'm Alex! I work at MIT Lincoln Lab on cislunar astrodynamics and space domain awareness sensing systems. I love space exploration, optics, computer vision, astrodynamics, propulsion, and experimental aircraft; you'll find examples of my projects, papers, and photography in the taskbar above. Outside of school & work, I also enjoy different kinds of exploration -- I'm an astrophotographer, wilderness backpacker, private pilot, cyclist, unicyclist, runner, windsurfer, sailor, SCUBA diver, and climber.

MIT Engineering Systems Lab -- Undergraduate Researcher

In this role, I performed technology portfolio development in support of NASA's upcoming 2023-2032 Heliophysics Decadal Survey. This includes learning about the engineering of scientific instruments for heliophysics missions, enumerating possible future development paths for the field as a whole, and using modern portfolio theory to rigorously rank technology development paths based on their risk and expected scientific return.

MIT Rocket Team: Liquid Propulsion Group

The team has been working on a DMLS-printed regeneratively-cooled LOX+ethanol engine, for which I contributed FEA and CFD analyses for the engine chamber and nozzle.

In previous semesters on the liquid propulsion group, we designed and built a 400 lbf. LOX+ethanol engine. I created the engine chamber, nozzle, and ignition system, along with various components of the test stand and fluids system.

ExoAnalytic Solutions Intern

For much of 2020 I was a full-time intern for ExoAnalytic. My work included three projects: systems engineering and astrodynamics analysis for a cislunar space situational awareness satellite constellation; creating a high-level design for a COTS space telescope for space situational awareness; and brainstorming ideas for future space traffic management architectures, both technical- and policy-wise, to guide ExoAnalytic's response to the space debris issue in LEO.

I have two conference papers & presentations that came out of this work:

Risk Maps for Conjunction Potential Throughout the Cislunar Domain, which was accepted for AMOS 2021 (The Advanced Maui Optical Space Surveillance Technologies Conference). This work computes the time it would take an object in any region of cislunar space to reach destinations where infrastructure resides, which provides a quantitative coverage weighting for potential cislunar space situational awareness systems.

Lessons Learned from FAA Drone Regulation for Space Traffic Management, which won the AAS Macauley Award in the Business & Space Policy Track, and which I presented at the Glenn Symposium in Summer 2021.

DaSH PA Human-Powered Airplane Team Member

DaSH PA is a 50 kg, 33-meter-wingspan human-powered airplane created by a team of volunteers in the SF Bay Area. I joined the team in 2017 and focused on the fabrication of low-weight components for the plane, including a new propellor, fuselage fairing, and wing sections. The pinnacle of my experience on the team was a winter 2018 trip to the deserts of Southern California to perform flight tests, where my components successfully completed several flights. Over the course of my time on the project, I acquired skills in low-weight & high-efficiency airplane design, learned to work with carbon fiber components, and gained my first real-world experience on an engineering team.

General Atomics Aeronautical Systems Intern

In Jan. 2019 I joined the effort at General Atomics to pave the way for integrating unmanned aerial vehicles into the national airspace system. Our team's goal was to demonstrate the ability of large UAVs to safely operate within controlled, heavily-trafficked airspace, including avoiding manned traffic, engaging in two-way communication with ATC, and performing collision avoidance maneuvers. I focused on the development of a particular cross-country flight demonstration at the heart of the effort, which involved creating risk mitigation methods for the flight and writing the flight demo CONOPS -- one of the key deliverables for the collaborating NASA UAS-NAS group in order to receive a flight waiver from the FAA. Through the course of my internship, I learned how policy and technical considerations interplay at the cutting edge of aerospace efforts.

MIT Dept. of Planetary Sciences Undergraduate Researcher

My research focused on finding possible physical traces left in the Oort Cloud by historical gravitational interactions between Oort Cloud objects and stellar flybys. This research is of interest to those who wish to understand possible origins of long-period comets and interstellar objects such as I1/2017 U1 ('Oumuamua) and 2I/2019 Q4 (Borisov). In my work, I utilized Gaia astrometrical data to identify historical stellar flybys, and wrote numerical simulations to study the dynamical affect of stellar perturbations on the Oort Cloud.

MIT Space Systems Lab Undergraduate Researcher

MIT SSL has been developing a remote sensing 3U cubesat telescope capable of achieving higher resolution than is typical for an aperture compact enough for such a small form factor, which is a key consideration in low-cost remote sensing systems. This cubesat (dubbed the SpinAp Telescope) features 30 cm x 10 cm rectangular mirrors that unfold into place in orbit, which then spin so as to emulate a full 70 cm diameter circular aperture in terms of resolution. The dynamics of pointing and slewing a rapidly-rotating telescope pose a unique challenge, however; my group's work was to prepare the testbed system for dynamics testing in the lab by proceduralizing the balancing of the testbed on the air bearing and validating gyroscopic and motor control. We also began initial work on dynamics testing, for which I created a ROS-enabled GUI to provide orientation control of the testbed system.
