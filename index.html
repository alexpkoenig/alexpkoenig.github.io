A bit about me...

Hello, I'm Alex! I currently study aerospace engineering and physics at MIT. I love space exploration, vision systems, astrodynamics, propulsion, and experimental aircraft; you'll find examples of my projects, papers, and photos in the taskbar above. Outside of school & work, I also enjoy different kinds of exploration -- I'm an astrophotographer, wilderness backpacker, private pilot, cyclist, runner, windsurfer, sailor, SCUBA diver, and climber.

What I've done
ExoAnalytic Solutions Intern

For much of 2020 I was a full-time intern for ExoAnalytic. My work included three projects: (1) assisting in developing an architecture for a satellite constellation intended to observe artificial satellites in cislunar space, including the relevant architectural analysis, trade studies, and simulations; (2) creating a high-level design for a company owned-and-built space telescope for providing space situational awareness to customers; and (3) brainstorming ideas for future space traffic management architectures, both technical- and policy-wise, to guide ExoAnalytic's response to the space debris issue in LEO.

DaSH PA Human-Powered Airplane Team Member

DaSH PA is a 50 kg, 33-meter-wingspan human-powered airplane created by a team of volunteers in the SF Bay Area. I joined the team in 2017 and focused on the fabrication of low-weight components for the plane, including a new propellor, fuselage fairing, and wing sections. The pinnacle of my experience on the team was when we drove the plane down to the deserts in Southern California to perform flight testing in the winter of 2018. During that memorable week, I saw the components I created successfully flown on several flights. Over the course of my time on the project, I acquired skills in low-weight & high-efficiency airplane design, learned to work with carbon fiber components, and gained my first real-world experience on an engineering team.

General Atomics Aeronautical Systems Intern

In Jan. 2019 I joined the effort at General Atomics to pave the way for integrating unmanned aerial vehicles into the national airspace system. Our team's goal was to demonstrate the ability of large UAVs to safely operate within controlled, heavily-trafficked airspace, including avoiding manned traffic, engaging in two-way communication with ATC, and performing collision avoidance maneuvers. I focused on the development of a particular cross-country flight demonstration at the heart of the effort, which involved creating risk mitigation methods for the flight, and writing the flight demo CONOPS -- one of the key deliverables for the collaborating NASA UAS-NAS group in order to receive a flight waiver from the FAA. Through the course of my internship, I learned how policy and technical considerations interplay at the cutting edge of aerospace efforts.

MIT Dept. of Planetary Sciences Undergraduate Researcher

My research focused on possible physical traces left in the Oort Cloud by historical gravitational interactions between Oort Cloud objects and stellar flybys. This research is of interest to those who wish to understand possible origins of long-period comets and interstellar objects such as I1/2017 U1 ('Oumuamua) and 2I/2019 Q4 (Borisov). In my work, I utilized Gaia astrometrical data to identify historical stellar flybys, and wrote numerical simulations to study the dynamical affect on the Oort Cloud resulting from the determined stellar perturbations.

MIT Space Systems Lab Undergraduate Researcher

MIT SSL has been developing a remote sensing 3U cubesat telescope capable of achieving higher resolution than is typical for an aperture compact enough for such a small form factor, which is a key consideration in low-cost remote sensing systems. This cubesat (dubbed the SpinAp Telescope) features 30 cm x 10 cm rectangular mirrors that unfold into place in orbit, which then spin so as to emulate a full 70 cm diameter circular aperture in terms of resolution. The dynamics of pointing and slewing a rapidly-rotating telescope pose a unique challenge, however; the work of my group (several freshmen at the time) was to prepare the testbed system for dynamics testing in the lab by proceduralizing the balancing of the testbed on the air bearing, validating gyroscopic and motor control, and providing documentation and training for the test setup. We also began initial work on dynamics testing, for which I created a GUI to provide orientation/spinning control of the testbed system.
